Why Unfollow Finder Only Ever Asks for 2 Files

Unfollow Finder Team 5 min read Updated July 2026

Finding out who unfollowed you on Instagram requires exactly one piece of information: a list of your followers, and a list of who you follow, at two different points in time. Compare them, and you have your answer. That's it — that's the whole problem.

So it's worth asking: why do some unfollower tracker tools ask you to upload your entire Instagram data export to do this?

What Instagram's full export actually contains

When you request your data from Instagram through Settings → Your Activity → Download Your Information, you're given a choice. You can select specific categories — like just your followers and following lists — or you can request everything: your messages, your photos and videos, your comments, your search history, your login activity, and more, all bundled into one archive.

For the specific task of finding unfollowers, only one of those categories is relevant. The rest has nothing to do with the question being asked.

What we noticed

We looked at how a few other unfollow-tracking tools handle this, including FollowBuddy. Their upload page asks users to submit their full Instagram ZIP export, with a stated maximum file size of 15GB — consistent with uploading the complete data archive, not just the followers and following lists.

Unfollow Finder's upload page, by contrast, asks for exactly two files: followers_1.json and following.json. Nothing else is requested, because nothing else is needed for the tool to do its job.

To be clear about what we can and can't say here: we don't have visibility into what happens on another company's servers after a file is uploaded, and we're not claiming anyone is misusing that data. What we can point to is simply what each tool asks for upfront — and asking for a full data export, messages and photos included, when the task only requires a follower list, is worth noticing regardless of what happens next.

Why this matters: the principle of least privilege

There's a well-established idea in data privacy and security called the principle of least privilege: a tool should only ever have access to the minimum data it needs to do its job, and nothing more. It's the same reason a flashlight app on your phone shouldn't need access to your contacts — needing more access than the task requires is a red flag, independent of whether that access is ever actually misused.

Applied here: a tool that only needs your followers and following lists to compare them shouldn't need your DMs, your photos, or your full activity history. The more data a tool asks for beyond what's functionally necessary, the more there is to worry about if that data is ever breached, sold, or mishandled down the line — even by accident.

What this means for you, practically

If you're choosing between unfollow-tracking tools, it's worth checking exactly what each one asks you to upload before you do it:

  • Ask what specific files or data categories the tool needs. If a tool can explain exactly which files it uses and why, that's a good sign.
  • Be cautious of tools that ask for "everything." If a tool asks for your complete data export without explaining why it needs more than a follower list, that's worth questioning.
  • Smaller uploads are also just simpler. Instagram's full data export for an active account can be several gigabytes; the two files needed to check unfollowers are typically just a few hundred kilobytes to a few megabytes, even for large accounts.
